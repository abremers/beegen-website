Does Your Small Business Really Need a Website in 2026?

Beegen Team April 22, 2026 5 min read

It's a fair question. You've got an Instagram account with a decent following. You're on Google Maps. Maybe you have a Facebook Business page that you update occasionally. Customers find you. Business is okay. So why bother with a website?

We hear this reasoning a lot, especially from local service businesses, solo freelancers, and early-stage founders. And in some narrow cases, they're right — you can operate without one. But the data paints a more complicated picture, and the argument gets harder to sustain the more seriously you want to be taken.

The Numbers First

75%
of consumers judge a business's credibility based on its website design
81%
of shoppers research a business online before making a purchase decision
46%
of all Google searches have local intent — people looking for businesses near them

That last number is the one most people underestimate. Nearly half of all Google searches are people actively looking for a business that can help them with something local — "plumber near me," "best coffee shop in [city]," "wedding photographer Austin." If you don't have a website, you're largely invisible to those searches.

What Instagram and Google Maps Can't Do

Social media and Google Business profiles are genuinely useful. We're not dismissing them. But they have structural limitations that a website doesn't.

You don't own the platform

Your Instagram account can be restricted, shadowbanned, or lose reach overnight due to an algorithm change you had no control over. Your Facebook page can be suspended. These platforms have happened to businesses that relied on them exclusively — sometimes catastrophically. A website is an asset you own. The content is yours, the URL is yours, and it's not subject to anyone else's terms of service changing without notice.

Social profiles don't rank in Google the way a website does

Instagram is a closed garden for Google. The posts you spend hours crafting contribute almost nothing to your search visibility. A website with good content can rank for searches that bring you customers for years — without you doing anything after the initial setup.

You can't control the presentation

On every social platform, your brand lives inside someone else's frame — their fonts, their layout, their ads alongside your content. A website lets you control exactly how your business is perceived from the first second a potential customer arrives.

There's no checkout, no booking, no contact form

If your Instagram bio says "DM for pricing," you're adding friction to every sale. A website can have a contact form, a booking widget, a services page with pricing, and a way for customers to take the next step without waiting for you to respond to a DM.

The "I'll Do It Later" Trap

Many business owners tell themselves they'll sort out the website once things are less busy, once they've got a logo sorted, once they know their offerings better. This is usually a mistake for one simple reason: the search engine clock doesn't start until you have a site.

Google's search ranking is, in part, a function of how long your site has existed and earned trust. A site you launch today starts building that history immediately. A site you launch in six months starts six months later. For businesses in competitive local markets, that gap matters.

When You Might Actually Be Fine Without One

To be fair: there are situations where the urgency is lower.

  • If you work exclusively through referrals and your pipeline is already full, a website is less critical in the short term.
  • If you're running a very early experiment and you're not sure the business model works yet, a landing page is probably sufficient over a full site.
  • If your entire business operates inside a marketplace (Etsy, Upwork, Amazon) and you have no desire to build a direct relationship with customers outside it.

But even in these cases, "I don't urgently need one right now" is different from "I don't need one." The referral pipeline dries up. The marketplace takes its cut and changes its algorithm. Having a home base on the internet is just a basic part of operating a serious business.

What It Costs to Not Have One vs. Having One

The old argument against websites was cost. A professionally built site ran $2,000–$10,000 upfront plus ongoing maintenance and hosting. For a small business, that's a real barrier.

That barrier is mostly gone. With AI website builders, a professional-looking site with your branding, a clear description of your services, and a contact form costs $19/month. That's less than most business owners spend on coffee in a week.

The question isn't "can I afford a website?" It's "can I afford not to have one?"
