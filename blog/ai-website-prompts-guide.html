Writing Prompts That Build Great Websites

Beegen Team April 22, 2026 5 min read

The most common frustration people have with AI website builders is a result that looks generic — like a template rather than a real business's website. The culprit is almost never the AI. It's the prompt.

An AI website builder is only as good as the brief you give it. Feed it vague input and you get a vague output. Give it a rich, specific description and it can make genuinely smart creative decisions about layout, copy, tone, and structure. Here's how to get into the second category.

The Six Ingredients of a Great Website Prompt

01

Business type and category

What kind of business is this? "Wedding photographer" is better than "photographer." "B2B SaaS for HR teams" is better than "software company."

02

Your target customer

Who is this site for? Age range, profession, problem they're trying to solve. The AI uses this to set the right tone and emphasis.

03

What makes you different

Your unique angle, specialisation, or value proposition. This is what goes in the headline and hero copy — don't let the AI guess.

04

Pages and sections needed

List the specific sections you need: homepage, about, services with pricing, portfolio, contact form, FAQ, testimonials, etc.

05

Tone and visual vibe

Professional and corporate? Warm and personal? Bold and edgy? Give the AI a direction for both the writing style and the visual feel.

06

Key details to include

Location, pricing anchors, specific services, credentials, years of experience — anything that should appear on the site.

You don't need all six in every prompt. But the more you include, the more the AI can do on your behalf without you having to fix things afterward.

Weak vs. Strong: Real Examples

Example 1: Personal trainer

A website for a personal trainer.
A website for a personal trainer based in Melbourne who specialises in strength training for busy professionals aged 30–50. My USP is that I offer 45-minute sessions at 6am and 7am before the workday starts. I need a homepage with a strong hero, a Services section (1-on-1 training, group sessions, online coaching), a Pricing section (from $95/session), client testimonials, and a booking CTA. Tone: motivating but not bro-ish — think "calm confidence." Dark colour scheme with orange accents.

Example 2: Architecture firm

Website for an architecture studio.
A portfolio website for a boutique architecture and interior design studio in Copenhagen. We do residential projects only — home extensions, renovations, and new builds. Our clients are homeowners with a budget of €150k+. The aesthetic should be Scandinavian minimalist: lots of white space, clean lines, a neutral palette with warm stone tones. Pages needed: Home (hero image area for project photography), Projects (portfolio grid), About (our story and team), and Contact. Copy should feel calm, considered, and premium — not corporate.

Example 3: SaaS product

A landing page for my app.
A marketing landing page for a B2B SaaS tool that helps restaurant managers schedule staff shifts and track labour costs. Target user: independent restaurant owners with 5–30 employees who are currently using spreadsheets or pen and paper. Key benefits: cuts scheduling time by 70%, reduces labour cost overruns, mobile-friendly for staff. Pricing starts at $49/month. I need a hero section, a "How it works" section (3 steps), a features section, a pricing table, and a CTA to start a free trial. Tone: practical and straightforward — owners are busy, not interested in hype.

How to Refine Without Burning Credits

If your first result is close but not quite right, you don't need to start over with a new credit. Use follow-up messages to make targeted changes:

  • "Make the hero headline more direct and benefit-focused" — refines copy without rebuilding the layout
  • "The pricing section feels too cluttered — simplify it to two tiers" — targeted structural edit
  • "Change the colour scheme to dark navy and gold" — reskins the whole site
  • "Add a FAQ section after the pricing, covering these 4 questions: [paste questions]" — adds a new section
  • "The tone feels too formal — rewrite the About section to sound more personal and conversational" — rewrites a specific section

Think of it like working with a designer: the first version is a starting point, and you're directing revisions. Each message refines the current site rather than rebuilding from scratch.

The One Thing Most People Forget

The single most overlooked element in website prompts is who the customer is and what they want to feel. Most people describe the business — what they offer, what they do — without describing the customer's perspective.

A dentist's website for nervous patients should feel very different from one targeting people who want cosmetic work. A children's tutoring service should feel different from executive coaching. When you tell the AI who the customer is and what they're hoping for, the copy and layout choices it makes are dramatically more on-target.
